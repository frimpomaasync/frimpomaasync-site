Notes from the build

blog.

Systems, strategy, and the real work of building in public. Take what you need, leave what you don't.

When I decided I needed a website, every single answer was the same: pick a platform. Squarespace. Shopify. Wix. Wordpress with seven plugins. Pay the monthly fee. Learn their dashboard. Squeeze your idea into their template until it sort of looks like you.

And I almost did it. I had the Squarespace tab open. I had my card ready. Then I stopped and asked myself a simple question: why am I about to rent a worse version of the exact thing I build for other people?

Because that's what those platforms are — rent. You don't own the thing. You own the right to keep paying for it.

I'm not anti-Squarespace. If you need a big store with a hundred products and inventory syncing, by all means. Those tools exist for a reason. But that's not what most of us actually need. What I needed was a clean front door. A page that said who I am, showed what I make, and let someone book a call or grab a tool. That's it.

What I actually wanted.

I sat down and wrote the list of what mattered. It was short:

  • It should feel like me, not a theme everyone else also uses
  • It should load instantly, even on a spotty phone signal
  • No monthly fee draining out of my account forever
  • I should be able to change a word without asking permission from a dashboard
  • It should be mine — something I own, not something I lease

Look at that list. Not one thing on it requires a platform. Every single one is actually easier without one.

So I built my own front door.

The site you're reading this on right now is the answer. It's a handful of simple pages I built and own outright. No subscription. No plugins to update. No theme to fight. When I want to change something, I open the file, change the word, and it's done. It costs me almost nothing to keep online.

And here's the part that still makes me smile: the whole site behaves like one of my tools — it builds itself in front of you as you scroll, because that's literally what I do for a living. I couldn't have done that on Squarespace. The thing that makes it mine is the exact thing a template would have flattened.

The goal was never "a website." The goal was a thing I own that does one job well. Turns out that's a much smaller, much friendlier problem than the internet makes it sound.

What this means for you.

If you've been putting off your own site because the whole platform decision feels heavy and expensive and locked-in — good news. You might not need any of that. You might just need a simple page that's yours, built around how you actually work, that you never have to pay rent on again.

That's the whole idea behind what I make. One simple tool you own. A front door included. No template, no subscription to me, no tech headache on your end. You tell me what you want it to do, and I build it around your life instead of forcing your life into someone else's box.
