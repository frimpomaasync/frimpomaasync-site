What automating a one-person business actually costs in 2026.

Five routes, real numbers, the costs nobody puts on the pricing page, and a break-even sum you can do on the back of an invoice. Including mine — priced against the others, not hidden from them.

NaNa Frimpomaa·22 July 2026·11 min read
The short answer

Expect $600 to $3,500 in year one, depending on route. All-in-one software is cheapest per month and most expensive in your hours. A done-for-you build is the reverse. The number that decides it isn't the price — it's how many recovered jobs a month it takes to cover the cost. For most solo owners that's under one.

Nobody gives you this table, so here it is. I build these systems for a living, my own price is in row five, and I've put it next to everything else on purpose. If one of the other rows is right for you, take it — you'll find out faster reading this than on a sales call with me.

The cost nobody quotes: your hours.

Every route above except two has a second price tag on it, and it's written in your evenings.

Configuring an all-in-one platform properly — pipelines, workflows, calendar sync, phone number, message deliverability, the booking page — is a 10 to 20 hour job for someone who has never done it. That's the industry's own estimate, not mine. Put your own hourly value against that. Most owner-operators I speak to price themselves somewhere between $50 and $75 an hour, which turns "free setup" into $500 to $1,500 of unpaid work before the first automated text goes out.

The other three that surprise people.

  • Usage fees on top of the plan. Texts, calls and email sends are usually metered separately. Budget another $10–$40 a month at solo volume, more if you're chasing a lot of quotes.
  • Business texting registration. To send texts from a business number in the US you register the number and the campaign. It's a small one-off plus a small monthly, and it's not optional — unregistered messages get filtered into nowhere.
  • The silent breakage. An automation that stops firing doesn't announce itself. It just gets quiet, and you assume it's a slow week. This is the real argument for a care plan, whoever you buy it from — someone has to be watching.
Software is only cheap if your time is free. It isn't — you're the one holding the business up.

The break-even sum.

Forget comparing prices to each other. Compare the price to one job. Three numbers, one minute:

  1. Your average job value. Not your best job. The middle one. Call it J.
  2. Your close rate on inquiries you actually reach. Half is a fair guess if you don't track it. Call it C.
  3. Multiply. J × C = what one recovered inquiry is worth to you.

Then divide the monthly cost by that number. That's how many extra jobs a month it has to save to pay for itself.

Questions owners ask about this.

Why is a one-time build more expensive than a month of software?
Because you're not paying for software, you're paying for the 10 to 20 hours of configuration plus the writing — every message in your voice, every rule about who gets chased and when. The software month is rent on an empty room. The build is the room furnished.
Can't I just get someone on a freelance site to do it cheaper?
Often yes, and for a simple booking flow that can be the right call. Ask two questions before you do: who fixes it in month three when a message stops sending, and do you get the account and the data if they disappear. Cheap plus unmaintained is the most expensive version of this.
What happens to the monthly cost if I stop paying it?
With mine, the build is already yours and the care plan is only monitoring, fixes and one improvement a month. Ask any provider the same question in writing before you pay, and make sure the answer includes exporting your customer data.
Is there a cheaper first step that isn't nothing?
Yes — the free phone route in the five-minute reply note. About an hour of setup, no cost, and it handles the acknowledgement half of the problem. Do that before you spend anything.
Do these numbers change outside the US?
The software and service prices are broadly similar; the texting registration rules are US-specific. Labour-based routes like a virtual assistant swing the most by region.
