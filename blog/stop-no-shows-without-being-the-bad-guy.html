How to stop no-shows without being the bad guy.

Three reminders, six messages you can copy, a deposit ask that doesn't feel like a threat, and the one thing to send when somebody has already stood you up.

NaNa Frimpomaa·22 July 2026·8 min read
The short answer

Send exactly three: a confirmation the second they book, a reminder 24 hours out that asks for a one-word reply, and a two-hour nudge. The reply is what matters — someone who just typed "yes" has made a commitment, and someone who doesn't reply has told you the slot is at risk while there's still time to refill it.

A no-show is almost never someone deciding not to come. It's someone who booked eleven days ago in a different mood, wrote it nowhere, and genuinely forgot until they saw your missed call. Which means the fix isn't firmness. It's presence — small, warm, and on a schedule.

Six messages you can copy.

01 · Confirmation, sent instantly
You're booked, [First name] — [day] [date] at [time], at [address]. It's [Name] coming out, and I'll text when I'm on my way. If anything changes just reply here, no drama.

"No drama" is doing real work. People ghost because cancelling feels rude. Make it cheap.

02 · Day-before reminder — the important one
Hi [First name] — still good for [time] tomorrow? Just reply Y or N so I know whether to hold the slot.

Short, one question, one keystroke. Long reminders get skimmed and forgotten.

03 · Two-hour nudge
On track for [time] today, [First name]. [Parking's easiest on the street / I'll ring the top bell / I'll text when I'm 10 minutes out.]

One practical detail makes it feel like a person, not a system.

04 · No reply by the evening before
Hi [First name] — haven't heard back so I'm going to offer [time] tomorrow to someone on the waiting list. Tell me before [8pm] and it's yours.

Not a threat — a fact with a deadline. This one converts surprisingly well.

05 · The deposit ask
Because this one's a [length] slot, I take a [$X] deposit to hold it — it comes straight off the final bill. Here's the link: [link]. Once that's in, the time's yours.

Name the reason and say it comes off the bill. Small beats large: its job is commitment, not compensation.

06 · They didn't show
Hi [First name] — I had you down for [time] today and didn't want to just leave it. Hope everything's alright. I've got [alternative day] at [time] free if you still want it.

Assume something went wrong. This recovers more bookings than any reminder of the policy — and the ones it doesn't recover, you were never getting anyway.

The deposit question.

Most solo owners either take no deposit at all or leap to a big one after a bad week. Both are wrong. The rule is simple: take a deposit when the slot is expensive to lose — long appointments, real travel time, materials bought in advance. Skip it when the slot is easy to refill.

  • Small, not large. Enough that skipping feels like a loss, not enough that booking feels like a risk.
  • Always name what it's for and always say it comes off the final bill. Silence about it reads as a fee.
  • Refund the first one, always. Someone's car broke down. Refund it, say something kind, and you've bought a customer for years.
  • Never introduce it mid-conversation as a punishment. It goes in the booking flow, same for everyone, or it doesn't go in at all.

A cancellation policy that doesn't scare people.

Paste into your booking page
I'm a one-person business, so an empty slot is a day I can't get back. [X hours]' notice and there's no charge at all — life happens. Less than that, or nobody home when I arrive, and I keep the deposit.

One number, one timeframe, one reason. No legal voice, no list of exceptions.

Nobody has ever cancelled on a business because the reminder was too warm.

Build it in twenty minutes.

  1. Turn on your booking tool's confirmation and reminder emails. Most free calendars do 24-hour and 1-hour reminders already. Switch them on, then rewrite the wording — the defaults are corporate and get ignored.
  2. Add texts. Reminder emails at 24 hours are fine; the two-hour nudge should be a text, because that's the one people actually see.
  3. Save messages 04 and 06 as phone shortcuts. They're the ones you'll never feel like typing in the moment.
  4. Keep a standby list. Three names who'd take a last-minute slot. Message 04 is worthless without it.
  5. Write the policy once, put it on the booking page, and never bring it up in conversation again.
Where the manual version breaks

It breaks the week you're busiest — which is exactly the week an empty slot hurts most. The 24-hour reminder goes out because your calendar does it; the reply nobody answered goes unnoticed because nobody is reading the replies. Then the slot sits empty and you find out at 2pm.

The reminders aren't the hard part. Watching who answered, and doing something about the ones who didn't — that's the part that needs to run without you.

Questions owners ask about this.

Isn't three messages a lot?
It reads as attentive, not pushy, because each one is short and only one asks for anything. What feels like a lot is four paragraphs of terms and conditions.
What if they confirm and still don't show?
Then it wasn't forgetfulness, and no cadence fixes that. Send message 06, offer one alternative, and if that's ignored, let them go. Repeat no-shows go on the deposit-required list quietly.
Should the reminders come from my number or a system?
Your number, or a business number people can reply to. A no-reply address for an appointment reminder throws away the confirmation you were asking for.
Do I do this for quotes as well as appointments?
Different job. Quotes need the follow-up ladder in the five-minute note. Reminders are only for time that's already been set aside.
What's the single change if I only do one thing?
Message 02 — the day-before reminder that asks for one word back. It surfaces the problem while you can still fix it.
