How to reply to every lead in five minutes when you're the only one there.

You are on a ladder, under a sink, mid-appointment, or driving. The lead does not care. Here is the five-minute reply window in plain terms, five texts you can copy today, and the ladder that runs after them.

NaNa Frimpomaa·22 July 2026·9 min read
The short answer

Reply inside five minutes — but split the reply in two. An acknowledgement goes out automatically the second a call is missed or a form is filled, saying exactly when you'll call back. The answer comes from you, when you're off the job. Most work is lost in the silence before the acknowledgement, not in the wait for the answer.

Every solo owner I talk to has the same picture in their head: a lead comes in, and somewhere in the next hour they are supposed to become a different person — one who checks their phone between jobs, remembers the name, and calls back before dinner. That person does not exist. You are working.

So the fix cannot be "try harder." It has to be a change in what a reply is.

What the research actually says about the window.

Two studies get quoted constantly. Here is what they found, without the scare framing.

The Lead Response Management study (Dr. James Oldroyd, 2007) looked at millions of inbound leads and found the odds of qualifying a lead fell off a cliff between a five-minute reply and a thirty-minute reply — roughly a 21-fold difference. Harvard Business Review ran a similar analysis in 2011 across 2,241 US companies and found firms that responded within an hour were about seven times more likely to have a meaningful conversation with a decision-maker than firms that waited just one hour longer, and about sixty times more likely than firms that waited a day.

Both studies are old enough to have grandchildren, and both keep getting re-confirmed because the reason behind them hasn't changed: a person looking for a plumber at 7pm is not shopping, they are solving. They will take the first competent answer that arrives. Being the first competent answer is a scheduling problem, not a sales problem.

The window isn't about being fast at selling. It's about being present at the exact minute someone decided to act.

Split the reply in two.

Here is the move that makes five minutes possible for one person. Stop treating "reply" as one thing. It's two:

The two halves of a reply
 The acknowledgementThe answer
JobStop them shopping. Prove a human exists.Price, availability, the actual conversation.
DeadlineUnder 5 minutesSame day
Who does itNever you. Automatic, every time.You, when you're free.
LengthTwo sentencesAs long as it needs

Once you separate them, the impossible standard disappears. You are not promising to be reachable in five minutes. You are promising to be accounted for in five minutes — which a phone can do while you're holding a wrench.

Five replies you can copy today.

Swap the bracketed bits for your own words. Keep them short — long automatic messages read as automatic.

01 · Missed call, during work hours
Hi, this is [Name] at [Business] — sorry I missed you, I'm on a job right now. I'll call you back by [time]. If it's urgent, text me what you need and I'll work it in.

Naming a real time is the whole trick. "ASAP" is what everyone says, so it reads as nothing.

02 · Missed call, after hours
Hi, this is [Name] at [Business]. You've reached me after hours — I'm not ignoring you, I'm just off the clock. I'll call you first thing at [time] tomorrow. If you tell me what's going on I can bring the right parts.

"I'm not ignoring you" does more work than any professional phrasing. It answers the thing they were actually wondering.

03 · Form or DM inquiry
Got it, [First name] — thanks for reaching out about [what they asked for]. I've got your details. I'll come back to you by [time] with [a price / two times that work / the answer]. Anything I should know before then?

The closing question keeps them typing, which is how you find out how serious they are before you spend an hour on a quote.

04 · Quote sent, no answer (day two)
Hi [First name] — just making sure that quote landed, they sometimes end up in junk. No pressure either way. If the number's wrong for you, tell me and I'll see what I can do differently.

Giving them permission to say no is what gets the reply. Most silence is embarrassment about price, not disinterest.

05 · The close-out (day seven)
Hi [First name] — I'll stop chasing you now, I know how it goes. If [the problem] comes back around, you've got my number and I'll keep your details on file. All the best either way.

This one gets replies more often than any of the others. Say it and mean it.

That's the same two-part shape on a screen: an acknowledgement that went out without anybody typing it, and a conversation that stayed alive long enough for a booking link to land. The owner was mid-appointment for all of it.

The ladder that runs after the first reply.

One reply is not follow-up. Follow-up is a small number of touches on a fixed schedule, and then a clean stop. Four to five, over about a week:

That last row is the one people forget, and it is the one that costs you. Nothing sours a booked customer faster than getting chased for a job they already said yes to.

What "one useful thing" means on day four.

  • A photo of a similar job you finished — "this is the same fix, took a morning"
  • The honest thing they should check themselves before paying anyone
  • What it costs if they wait until it gets worse — stated plainly, no drama
  • A slot that just opened up, if one genuinely did

How to build this today for free.

About an hour of setup. No subscription. Do this before you consider paying anyone, including me.

  1. Turn on missed-call auto-text. Most business phone apps and both major phone systems can send a text automatically when a call goes unanswered. Paste script 01 in.
  2. Write a second version for after hours and switch between them manually at the end of the day, or set a schedule if your phone allows it. Script 02.
  3. Make your form send an instant confirmation. Every free form tool does this. Paste script 03 into the auto-response.
  4. Save scripts 04 and 05 as text shortcuts on your phone. Type a short code, the whole message appears.
  5. Put two recurring reminders in your calendar — one mid-morning, one late afternoon — that just say "who's waiting on me?"
  6. Keep one list of everyone who's mid-ladder, with a date column. A spreadsheet is fine. The list is what makes the ladder real.
Where the free version breaks

It breaks at step 6, always. The automatic parts run fine — it's the remembering that fails. Day two arrives while you're on a job, day four never happens, and the day-seven close-out that would have won the work never goes out. Free automation can send a message. It can't know who already answered, who's booked, and who's been sitting untouched since Tuesday.

That's the difference between a phone trick and a system: a system holds the list, moves people along it, and shuts up the moment they book.

Questions owners ask about this.

Doesn't an automatic text feel impersonal?
Only when it's written like a robot wrote it. Read scripts 01 and 02 out loud — they sound like a person who is busy, because that's what you are. The rule is never to pretend the automatic message is you typing live. Say what happens next, then do it.
What if I reply fast and they still don't book?
Then you found out fast, which is worth something. Speed doesn't make people buy things they don't want. It decides who gets the shot at the people who do.
How many follow-ups is too many?
More than five in a week starts to feel like pressure. The ladder above is four automatic touches plus your real answer, ending in a clean close-out. Ending well is what makes them call you next time.
I already miss calls all day. Where do I start?
Script 01 and nothing else. One message, turned on today. Add the rest next week. Half-built and running beats fully planned and off.
Does this work for booked appointments too?
Same shape, different messages — confirm on booking, remind the day before, remind two hours out. That's covered in the no-show note.
