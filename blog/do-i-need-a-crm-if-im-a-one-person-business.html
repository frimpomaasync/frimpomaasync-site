Do I need a CRM if I'm a one-person business?

Probably not yet. Here's the nine-column sheet that does the same job for free, the four statuses that matter, the ten-minute review that makes it work, and the exact point where paying for software starts making sense.

NaNa Frimpomaa·22 July 2026·8 min read
The short answer

No — you need one list that answers one question: who is waiting on me right now? Nine columns in a spreadsheet does that for free. A CRM earns its money later, at roughly thirty open conversations, when repeat business becomes a real share of income, or the day someone else starts answering your messages.

I build systems for a living and I'll still tell you this: most solo owners who buy a CRM stop using it within six weeks. Not because the software is bad. Because it asked them to maintain a picture of their business that was more detailed than the business itself.

Then the work goes back to living in three places — your texts, your head, and a note on the van dashboard — which is where it was leaking from in the first place.

The problem was never that you lacked a database. It's that nobody is asking, on a Tuesday, who's been waiting since Friday.

The two columns most people leave off are source and next action date — and they're the two that make the sheet worth keeping. Without source, you'll keep paying for the advertising that isn't working. Without a next action date, the sheet is a graveyard: an accurate record of everyone you meant to call.

The four statuses, and nothing more.

  • New — they've asked, you haven't answered properly yet
  • Quoted — the ball is in their court
  • Booked — it's in the calendar. Stop chasing immediately
  • Closed — done or dead. Which one goes in the notes

Anyone who tells you to add "nurturing" or "warm" is describing a sales team's problem. You'll spend more time grooming statuses than calling people.

The ten-minute weekly review.

Pick a fixed slot — Monday morning with coffee, Friday before you shut down. Same time every week. Then:

  1. Sort by next action date, oldest first. Everything with a date in the past is a person currently waiting on you.
  2. Work top down. Send the message, then immediately set a new next action date. Never leave the cell blank — a blank cell is how someone disappears.
  3. Anything Quoted for more than seven days gets the close-out message and moves to Closed. Ending it cleanly is what gets you the callback next year.
  4. Glance at the source column. Once a month, notice which one keeps producing. Then do more of that and stop paying for the rest.
  5. Close the sheet. Ten minutes. That's the entire system.
The honest weakness

A spreadsheet cannot remind you. It has no opinion about the quote you sent nine days ago and it will never tell you that Tuesday's caller has been sitting untouched since Friday. Every bit of its usefulness rests on you opening it on a day you're already too busy.

Which is exactly the week it stops happening — and the week you can least afford it to. That gap is the only real argument for automation. Not features. Not dashboards. Just: something has to notice on the weeks you can't.

Questions owners ask about this.

What about free CRMs? They cost nothing.
They cost nothing in money and quite a lot in attention. A free CRM is still a second place to keep things up to date, with fields you didn't choose. If you want to try one, keep the sheet running alongside for a month and see which one you actually open.
Where should the sheet live?
Somewhere that opens on your phone in under five seconds, standing outside a job. If it's a desktop file you open at night, you'll update it from memory, and memory is what the sheet was for.
Do I put existing customers in it too?
Only if you plan to contact them again on a schedule. Otherwise keep it to open conversations — the sheet's job is who's waiting, not who exists.
How is this different from my notes app?
One column: next action date. That's the only thing turning a list of names into something that tells you what to do. Notes apps hold information; this sorts it.
Can I have the sheet already built?
Build it yourself — it's nine words and it'll take you four minutes, and the four minutes is what makes you use it. If you'd rather have the whole thing filling itself in as leads arrive, that's the Client Catcher.
